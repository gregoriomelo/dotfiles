# Reference Library

Exemplars to draw from and the specific lessons each one teaches.

---

## Stripe

**What they do:** Financial infrastructure presented with the clarity of editorial design.

**Specific lessons:**
- Dense information made scannable through generous line height and section spacing
- Color used conservatively — almost entirely neutral with precisely placed purple accents
- Tables that breathe: plenty of row height, clear header differentiation, right-aligned numbers
- Documentation that feels like a product, not an afterthought
- Gradient meshes used as brand expression on marketing, nowhere in product UI

**When to reference:** Data-dense interfaces, API/developer documentation, pricing tables, financial dashboards, any context where trust and clarity are paramount.

**Key technique to steal:** Their use of subtle card elevation — a 1px border plus a very faint shadow, never both loud. Cards feel defined without feeling heavy.

---

## Linear

**What they do:** Project management UI that feels like it was designed by engineers who love design.

**Specific lessons:**
- Dark mode done seriously: multiple surface levels, careful use of color temperature
- Keyboard-first UX reflected in visual design — actions feel efficient, nothing decorative
- Typography at small sizes with precise optical adjustments
- Motion that communicates state, never decorates
- Minimal chrome — the content is the interface

**When to reference:** Dark-mode UIs, tool-like products, dashboards for power users, anything where efficiency signals trust.

**Key technique to steal:** Their sidebar — extremely low visual weight, high information density, clear active states using subtle background fill rather than border or underline.

## Apple (Human Interface Guidelines + Marketing)

**What they do:** Consumer technology with craft at every scale.

**Specific lessons:**
- Whitespace as a luxury signal — things given room feel more valuable
- Photography as a design element, not decoration: full bleed, high contrast, single subject
- Type at display sizes treated as sculpture
- The "one more thing" principle: every layout has one primary action or message
- System integration: respecting safe areas, following platform conventions as a form of quality

**When to reference:** Consumer-facing products, presentations that need to feel premium, any context where the audience is evaluating quality before evaluating content.

**Key technique to steal:** Their headline/subhead pairing — a large, bold, short headline with a lighter, slightly smaller subhead positioned very close to it. The proximity and weight contrast create strong hierarchy.

---

## Bauhaus

**What they do:** Early 20th century German design school — the foundation of modern visual design.

**Specific lessons:**
- Form follows function: every element serves a purpose
- Primary geometry: circles, squares, triangles as organizing principles
- Primary colors + black + white: a palette that is complete and restrained
- Grid as the invisible structure holding everything together
- Craft at every scale — even the binding of a book matters

**When to reference:** Infographics, data visualization, presentations that need to feel authoritative and timeless, brand systems.

**Key technique to steal:** The Bauhaus poster grid — a strong vertical rule dividing a 1/3 label column from a 2/3 content column. Creates instant visual order.

---

## Swiss International Typographic Style

**What they do:** Mid-century Swiss designers who systematized how information should be presented.

**Specific lessons:**
- The grid is everything — every element placed relative to a defined baseline and column system
- Helvetica/Aktiv/Neue Haas: neutral typefaces let content speak
- Flush left, ragged right body text as the default
- Photography cropped tight to the subject
- Asymmetric balance — no centering unless there's a specific reason

**When to reference:** Annual reports, corporate presentations, any document that needs to project authority and clarity.

**Key technique to steal:** The asymmetric title page — title set in the top-left third, author in the bottom-right third, everything else empty. The negative space is the design.

## Dieter Rams (Braun Design System)

**What his work does:** Industrial design translated to interface principles.

**Ten Principles (apply to visual design):**
1. Good design is innovative
2. Good design makes a product useful
3. Good design is aesthetic
4. Good design makes a product understandable
5. Good design is unobtrusive
6. Good design is honest
7. Good design is long-lasting
8. Good design is thorough down to the last detail
9. Good design is environmentally friendly
10. **Good design is as little design as possible**

**When to reference:** When you're about to add something — ask "Would Rams approve?" If you can't justify it, remove it.

## Edward Tufte (Data Visualization)

**What he does:** The definitive authority on presenting data visually.

**Core principles:**
- **Data-ink ratio** — maximize data, minimize ink. Remove every mark that doesn't encode information.
- **Chartjunk** — grids, borders, background fills, 3D effects, unnecessary legends. All harmful.
- **Small multiples** — the same chart, repeated at small scale across many instances, for comparison
- **Direct labeling** — label data directly rather than using a legend requiring eye travel
- **The table** — often better than the chart. Don't chart when a table is clearer.

**When to reference:** Any data visualization, dashboard, analytics view, financial summary.

**Key technique to steal:** Remove the y-axis entirely and label the data points directly. Data-ink ratio jumps immediately.
