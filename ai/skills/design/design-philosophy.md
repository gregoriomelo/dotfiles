# Design Philosophy

## Core Beliefs

**Restraint is a skill, decoration is a crutch.**
Every visual element should earn its place. If you can't articulate why something exists, remove it. The best design often comes from what you decided not to include.

**Tension creates interest.**
Perfect harmony is forgettable. The best work holds deliberate tension: heavy type against generous whitespace, a single saturated accent in a muted palette, a grid broken exactly once for emphasis. Monotony signals a lack of decision-making.

**Systems over one-offs.**
A design decision applied once is arbitrary. Applied consistently, it becomes a system. Before making any choice, ask: "Can this scale? Would this hold across 20 more screens?"

**Details are the difference.**
The gap between good and great is almost always in the details that most people never consciously notice: the tracking on uppercase labels, the optical correction on icon alignment, the extra 2px of breathing room around a button label. These details are felt even when they're not seen.

**Hierarchy is your primary tool.**
Before color, before animation, before illustration — hierarchy. A clear visual hierarchy tells the reader exactly where to look and in what order. If everything competes for attention, nothing has it.

## What to Avoid

- **Gradient abuse**: Gradients are a crutch. Use them only when they add meaning (depth, energy) not decoration.
- **Shadow inflation**: Box shadows that look like the element is floating in space. Shadows should suggest elevation, not drama.
- **Color soup**: More than 3 colors in a UI is almost always a mistake. Add a 4th only if the first 3 demand it.
- **Forced roundness**: Border radius should match the character of the content — technical tools have tighter corners, consumer products have rounder ones. Don't default to pill buttons everywhere.
- **Fake motion**: Animations that exist to look impressive, not to communicate state or guide attention.
- **Symmetry for its own sake**: Asymmetric layouts are more interesting and often more functional. Don't center everything.

## Questions to Live By

1. Does this element serve the user or the designer?
2. What happens if I remove this?
3. Is this decision consistent with every other similar decision?
4. Would a design director at Stripe accept this?
5. Is the visual hierarchy immediately obvious to a first-time viewer?
